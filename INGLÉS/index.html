Conditionals

Conditional forms have two parts: conditions and results.

IF + condition, result

* The order of the parts doesn't matter as long as “IF” stays within the condition part.

Zero Conditional

The Zero Conditional is used to express universal facts. 100% probability. Things that will pass no matter what happens.

We also use it to ask for something or give an order.

  • IF + condition (present simple), result (present simple)
  • "If you heat the water, it boils"

    • * ORDERS: this conditional is also used to give orders.

  • "If you go to the store, buy some bread."
  • "If you see my dad, don't tell him that i'm here."
  • "If you heat water to 100 degrees Celsius, it boils.
  • "If you mix blue and yellow, you get green.
  • "If I exercise in the morning, I have more energy throughout the day.

First Conditional

The first conditional is used to express future sceneries that have 90% of probability to happen. It is used to express plans.

IF + condition (Present Simple), WILL + result (Present Simple)

Example:

  • "If they are late to to the meeting, we will miss the screening."
  • "If you don't hurry up, we will not catch the bus"
  • If it rains tomorrow, I will bring my umbrella.
  • If you study hard, you will pass the exam.
  • If he doesn't leave now, he will miss the bus.

It can also be formed with WHEN instead of IF.

* WHEN = IF : When + condition, will + result

WHEN + contition (present simple), WILL + resultado (present simple)

  • When she stops talking, we will get to the next subject
  • When the man comes around, the animals will not have a place to stay

Second Conditional

The Second Conditional is used to talk about hypothetical or unlikely situations and their possible results. Like things you see in dreams or thoughts you had about a topic but never did.

Hypothetical imaginary scenarios. It is used to pose unrealistic ideas of the present or actions in the future that are somewhat probable. Imaginative.

IF + condition (Past Simple), WOULD + result (infinitive without "to")

Example:

  • If I won the lottery, I would buy a house.
  • If I studied, I would pass the exam.
  • You would speak japanese if I studied.
  • If I visited New York, I would see the Empire State.
  • You wouldn't work eight hours, if you were rich.
  • If I won the lottery, I would buy a new car.
  • If she had more free time, she would travel more.
  • If they were rich, they would donate to charity.

Relative clauses

Relative clauses are pronouns that connect relative sentences. A relative sentence is a kind of subordinate sentence, they are sentences related between each other.

There are two types:

  • Defining relative clauses(oracion subordinada especificativa): The information is necessary for the sentence to have meaning or sense. They don’t have commas. Example: The cake that has been served is made of chocolate. La tarta que ha sido servida está hecha de chocolate.
  • Non-defining relative clauses(oraciones subordinadas explicativas): The information isn't necessary for the sentence to have meaning or sense. They have commas. Example: The cake, which is made of chocolate, has been served. La tarta, que está hecha de chocolate, ha sido servida.

Pronouns:

  • who: This is used when referring to people. It can be used either as the subject of the object of any sentence. Example: The girl who works at the police station is my sister. Example: The man who acts in this movie is my favorite actor. El hombre que actúa en esta película es mi actor favorito. Example: Mario’s brother, who wears green overalls, is called Luigi. El hermano de Mario, que viste un mono verde, se llama Luigi.
  • Which: This is used when referring to animals and things. It can be used either as the subject or the object of any sentence. Example: The dog which is very friendly barks every night. Example: The dog which bit Mario's brother was a golden retriever El perro que mordió al hermano de Mario era un golden retriever Example: My brother’s phone, which is an iPhone, was very expensive El teléfono de mi hermano, que es un iPhone, fue muy caro
  • That: people, animals or things. It can replace the who and which in some case.
    • Example: The car tah is at the garage is mine. Example: The store that is at the end of this street has very good deals! La tienda que está al final de la calle tiene muy buenas ofertas Example: The man that is talking right now has very bad breath El hombre que está hablando ahora mismo tiene muy mal aliento
  • whose: It is used when describing a relationship of possession between people, animals or objects. Example: The girl whose phone was lost was crying so loud. Example: You want to know which dog is Mario’s? It’s the one whose collar is red ¿Quieres saber cuál es el perro de Mario? Es aquel cuyo collar es rojo Example: The journalist, whose articles talked about my town, is a famous person El periodista cuyos artículos hablaron sobre mi pueblo es una persona muy famosa
  • where: It is used when describing places. It can only be used when those places act as spots you visit/inhabit and not as objects. Example: the city where he was born is Cuenca. Example: The house where we lived was haunted La casa donde vivíamos estaba embrujada Example: The park, where we play every Sunday, is in works El parque, donde jugamos todos los domingos, está en obras
